The most accessible income method for most people in this course — especially those who do not have their own business yet — is providing content services to local businesses. Let me explain exactly how this works, step by step.

Think about a small local business: a clothing shop, a restaurant, a coaching center, a hardware store, a beauty salon. What do almost all of them have in common? They are on Facebook, but they post irregularly. Their posts are often low quality. They have no real strategy. And the owner wishes they could do better — but has no time.

You come in as the solution. For a fixed monthly fee — let us say Rs 2,500 to Rs 5,000 — you will manage their Facebook page. You will write 10–15 posts per month. You will create offers, announcements, and engaging content. You will be their "digital person."

How long does this take you each month, really? With AI, 10–15 posts take you about 2 to 3 hours total. Think about that. Rs 3,000 for 3 hours of work. That is Rs 1,000 per hour. Comfortable in any market.

And once you have the system running, it gets faster. You have templates. You have prompt formulas. You know the client's products. Month two and three take you less than 1.5 hours. Your effective hourly rate goes up while your effort goes down.

Now — what should you offer? Here is a simple service menu that works well in Nepal:

Basic Package (Rs 2,000–2,500/month): 8 Facebook posts per month, basic design advice, one offer per month.
Standard Package (Rs 3,500–4,500/month): 15 posts, 2 promotional offers, customer reply templates, monthly strategy report.
Premium Package (Rs 6,000–8,000/month): Daily posting plan, TikTok content, product descriptions, WhatsApp broadcast messages, monthly review meeting.

Start with Basic. Get 2–3 clients. Learn what they need. Then upgrade your service as you become more confident. Many students get to Rs 15,000 per month within 3 months — just from 4–5 small local clients on Basic or Standard packages.

Why this works
✓ It is respectful and non-pushy · ✓ Offers value first (free sample) · ✓ Mentions a clear, affordable price · ✓ Removes risk ("stop anytime") · ✓ Ends with a simple yes/no question

What to say face to face — practice this at home first

Step 1 — Build the connection first (1–2 minutes)
"Namaste [name] ji, I buy from your shop sometimes / I walk past your shop every day. I love what you sell here." Be genuine. Do not jump straight into the pitch.

Step 2 — Plant the idea (30 seconds)
"I noticed your Facebook page has not posted recently. I actually help small businesses with their Facebook content. Not expensive at all — and it really brings in new customers."

Step 3 — Offer proof immediately (1 minute)
"In fact, I prepared a sample post for your shop just to show you what I can do." Show them the post on your phone. Watch their reaction. Most will be impressed.

Step 4 — Make it easy to say yes
"If you like this, I can do 10–15 posts like this every month for Rs 3,000. No upfront payment — pay after the first month if you are happy. Shall we try one month?"

Create a free sample that makes them say "YES" immediately

Before you approach any client, create a free sample post for THEIR specific business. This is the single most powerful thing you can do. Here is how:

1. Look at their Facebook page (or their shop if they have none). Note: what do they sell? What is their price range? Who are their customers?

2. Open ChatGPT and type a strong RICE prompt using their actual products and details.

3. Get the post. Screenshot it. Copy it into a WhatsApp message.

4. Send the sample with the message: "I made this for your shop for free — use it today if you like. If you want more like this every week, I can do that for a small monthly fee."

The sample does all the selling for you. People do not buy services they imagine. They buy services they can already see working.

Selling on Facebook & TikTok with AI — Zero Investment, Real Money
⏱ 30 min 📱 Facebook + TikTok 💸 Start with Rs 0

Full Lesson — Read or Watch

Here is a business model that requires zero investment, zero inventory, and zero staff. Just your phone, ChatGPT, and the knowledge to use them. It works like this:

You find products being sold wholesale in Nepal — handmade crafts, clothing, electronics, food items, jewellery. You take photos (or get photos from the supplier). You use ChatGPT to write compelling product descriptions and posts. You post them on Facebook Marketplace and your personal/business profile with your own price markup. When someone buys, you collect the money, order from the supplier, and deliver. You keep the difference.

This is called "drop-shipping" or "reselling" — and people in Nepal are quietly making Rs 15,000 to Rs 40,000 per month doing this from their bedroom. The advantage of AI is simple: your posts look more professional, more persuasive, and more trustworthy than competitors who write generic descriptions.

On TikTok, the model is slightly different. You create short videos showing the product — just your phone camera, decent lighting from a window, and yourself or someone else using the product. Then you use AI to write the video caption, the call-to-action, and the script for what you will say. TikTok rewards consistency and authenticity — not production quality. A 30-second phone video with a genuine personality behind it beats a Rs 50,000 professional ad every time.

The key to making this work is picking the right products. Choose products that: have a clear visual appeal (they look good in photos), solve a common problem, are not easily available in local physical stores, and have at least 40–50% margin for you. Handmade Nepali products, locally-made clothing, and organic food items work exceptionally well.

Common Mistakes to Avoid
✗
Posting without any real photos — only text or stock images
In Nepal's Facebook buying culture, customers want to see the actual product. Stock images from the internet feel fake and reduce trust dramatically. Even a slightly blurry photo of the real item beats a professional stock photo. Take real photos — your phone camera is enough.

Fiverr is a website where people from all over the world buy services starting at $5 (roughly Rs 650). One important note: Fiverr takes 20% commission from every order. Factor this into your pricing. And you need a bank account or payment method (eSewa, bank transfer, or Payoneer) to receive your money in Nepal.

Here is something most people do not realise: you do not need to know everything to teach something. You only need to be a few steps ahead of the person you are teaching.

Right now, after completing four modules of this course, you know more about AI than 95% of people in your neighbourhood. Most people around you have never opened ChatGPT. They do not know what a prompt is. They are scared of technology. You — right now — have enough knowledge to teach a group of beginners everything they need to get started.

The WhatsApp Teaching Model works like this: You create a WhatsApp group. You charge a small monthly or one-time fee — Rs 300 to Rs 1,000 per person — to join. Inside the group, you share daily tips, voice notes, screenshots of useful AI tricks, exercises, and answer questions. Questions & Answers

You are probably wondering...

Is it honest to charge clients money when AI does most of the writing? +
Absolutely yes — and here is why. A plumber uses electric drills, pipe cutters, and modern tools. You still pay for the plumber's knowledge, judgment, time, and professionalism — not the cost of the tools. You are doing exactly the same. You bring: understanding of the client's business, knowledge of what makes a good post for their audience, quality review of every piece before delivery, and ongoing management and communication. AI is your tool. Your knowledge and judgment are your service. This is 100% honest and professional.
What if a potential client asks "How do you write so fast?" Should I mention AI? +
You can be honest without making it a sales objection. You can say: "I use modern writing tools and technology — the same tools used by the best content agencies in the world. What matters is the quality you get and the results for your business." You are not obligated to explain your full workflow to every client, any more than a chef explains every kitchen tool they use. Focus on the result: "Does the content work for your business?"
I live in a small town. Are there really enough businesses to make this work? +
Yes — often more than in cities, because competition is lower. In a small town, you may be the only person offering this service. Every local shop, school, hotel, clinic, restaurant, coaching center, and small business is a potential client. You only need 5 clients to earn Rs 12,500 to Rs 20,000 per month. Are there 5 businesses in your town with a Facebook page that could be better? Almost certainly. And once you have results to show, clients will refer more clients.
I am scared of rejection. What if everyone says no? +
Rejection is normal, expected, and not personal. Out of 10 people you pitch, expect 6 or 7 to say no — that is true for professional salespeople at major companies. The key insight is this: rejection is not a sign that your service is bad. It is a sign that you need to contact more people. If 3 out of 10 say yes, that is a successful business. And the more you pitch, the more comfortable you become, and your "yes rate" naturally improves. Start with warm contacts — people who already know and trust you — and rejection will be much rarer.
How do I handle payment? Do I need a registered business? +
For starting out with local clients, you do not need a registered business. Collect payment via eSewa, Khalti, or direct bank transfer — these are simple and legal. Ask for payment at the start of each month, or half upfront and half on delivery. For Fiverr/Upwork international income, you will need Payoneer (free to sign up) or a bank account that accepts international transfers. As your income grows, you may want to register as a freelancer or sole proprietorship — but that is a problem for month 6, not month 1.
What if a client wants a service I do not know how to do yet? +
Two good options: First, be honest — "I do not offer that service yet, but I know someone who might help." Honesty builds long-term trust that is worth more than one awkward sale. Second, learn it quickly — if the service is something ChatGPT can help with, ask ChatGPT how to do it, practice once, and if you feel confident, offer it. Never promise something you cannot deliver. But also do not underestimate how quickly you can learn new AI-assisted skills when there is a real client waiting.
