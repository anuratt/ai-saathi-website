Lesson 01 · Foundation
AI as Your Personal Research Assistant — Stop Wasting Hours Searching
⏱ 22 min🔍 Research Skills⚡ Saves 5+ hours per week
01
🎯
Lesson Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will know how to use ChatGPT as a personal research assistant — getting deep, useful information on any business or life topic in minutes instead of hours.
📖
Full Lesson

Let me paint you a picture. It is 10 PM. You are tired after a long day at the shop. But a thought has been bothering you: your neighbour just opened a similar business and seems to be attracting more customers. You want to understand what they are doing differently — their pricing, their products, their promotions. How do you find out?

Before AI, you had two bad options: spend hours searching Google and reading through dozens of websites, hoping something useful appears — or simply not bother and keep guessing. Both options are terrible when you are running a real business with real problems to solve.

With AI, you have a third option. You ask, in plain language, and you get a clear, thoughtful answer in 30 seconds. Not a list of irrelevant search results. Not a confusing article written for someone else. A direct, useful response to your exact question.

Think of ChatGPT as a consultant who has read millions of business books, studied thousands of industries, and worked with businesses all over the world — and who will answer your specific question for free, at any hour, without judgment or impatience.

The key insight of this lesson is: the quality of what you get from AI research depends entirely on the quality of your question. A vague question gets a vague answer. A specific, well-framed question gets a specific, actionable answer that you can use immediately.

The Research Question Formula

Before asking AI any research question, frame it using this three-part formula:

Part 1 — Context: Who you are and what your situation is.
Part 2 — The specific question: Exactly what you want to know.
Part 3 — What you want from the answer: A list? A comparison? A recommendation? A step-by-step plan?

Most people only do Part 2 and wonder why the answer is not useful. Parts 1 and 3 are what make the answer specific to YOUR situation.

Research vs. Google: What is the difference?

Google gives you a list of websites. You then have to open 10 of them, read through them, filter out the bad ones, and try to piece together an answer yourself. This takes 30–60 minutes for a complex question.

ChatGPT reads those websites for you (metaphorically), synthesises the key information, and tells you what you need to know in plain language in 30 seconds. It is not a replacement for your own judgment — but it is an extraordinary starting point that saves you enormous time.

One important note: for very recent news or local price data (today's vegetable prices in Kalimati, the current exchange rate), ChatGPT may not be fully up-to-date. For those specifics, combine AI research with a quick local check. But for strategic questions, business frameworks, market understanding, and planning — AI is exceptionally reliable and useful.

Common Mistakes to Avoid
✗
Accepting AI research results without any verification
AI is a brilliant starting point — not the final word. For important business decisions involving significant money, always verify key facts with local sources, actual competitors, or real customers. Ask AI: "How can I verify this information locally?" It will give you good suggestions.
✗
Asking vague questions and being disappointed by general answers
"How can I grow my business?" is too vague. "I sell handmade pottery in Bhaktapur, mostly to tourists, and I want to grow my online sales to Nepali diaspora abroad — what are the best 3 platforms and how do I start?" is specific. The more you tell AI about your exact situation, the more useful its answers become.
✗
Researching everything but acting on nothing
Some students become research addicts — they ask AI question after question, learn many things, but implement nothing. Knowledge without action has zero value. Every research session should end with you writing down 1 to 3 specific things you will do differently. Research that does not change your actions is just entertainment.

Lesson 02 · Customer Intelligence
Know Your Customer Better Than They Know Themselves
⏱ 28 min👥 Customer Understanding💰 Directly increases sales
02
🎯
Lesson Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will have a complete AI-generated profile of your ideal customer — including their deepest desires, real fears, decision-making habits, and the exact words they use — so you can speak to them in a way that feels personal and compelling.
📖
Full Lesson

Here is a hard truth about most small businesses: they talk about themselves instead of talking about their customers.

Their Facebook posts say: "Best quality! Lowest price! Come visit us!" But their customers are thinking: "Will this fit me? Is it genuine? Will they replace it if something goes wrong? Will I feel embarrassed if I buy a cheap thing in front of my friends?"

The business is talking about features. The customer is thinking about feelings, fears, and social status. There is a complete mismatch — and that mismatch costs you sales every single day.

The businesses that sell more are not necessarily selling better products. They are speaking more directly to what their customers actually feel and want. This is called customer understanding — and until recently, it took expensive research, focus groups, and marketing consultants to get it right.

With AI, you can now build a deep, accurate picture of your customer in 20 minutes, completely free. AI has read millions of studies, articles, social media posts, and business books about what different types of people want and how they make decisions. You can access all of that knowledge simply by asking the right questions.

What you are creating is called a Customer Profile — also known as a "buyer persona." It is a detailed description of one specific type of person who buys from you. Not all customers at once — just the one typical person you are trying to reach most. When you know that person deeply, everything becomes easier: your Facebook posts, your WhatsApp messages, your pricing, your promotions, your shop layout, even the way you greet people.

What a Customer Profile includes

Demographics: Age, gender, occupation, income level, family situation, location.
Desires: What are they really hoping for when they walk into your shop or see your post? What dream are they trying to reach?
Fears and frustrations: What worries them? What bad experiences have they had before? What makes them hesitate?
How they decide: Do they decide quickly on price, or slowly on trust? Do they ask friends first? Do they compare options online?
Their own language: What exact words and phrases do they use when talking about this product or service? Using their language in your posts makes your content feel like you are speaking directly to them.

Once you have this profile, you have a compass. Every piece of content you create, every offer you make, every message you send can be aimed precisely at this person — and it will resonate far more powerfully than generic communication that tries to speak to everyone.

👤
The Customer Profile Master Prompt
Customer Intelligence Prompt
→ I sell [your product/service] in [your city/area], Nepal. My typical customer is a [age range] [man/woman/person] who [brief description of their life — job, family, income level]. Please create a detailed customer profile for me that includes: 1. Their deepest desires and dreams related to [your product category] 2. Their biggest fears, frustrations, and hesitations when buying [your product] 3. How they make their buying decision — what factors matter most to them 4. What they have been disappointed by in the past with similar products/services 5. The exact words and phrases they would use to describe what they want 6. What would make them choose ME over a competitor 7. What would make them become a loyal, repeat customer 8. A realistic example of what they say to a friend when recommending a business like mine Be very specific. Think about the reality of daily life in Nepal for this person.
What you get back
A complete psychological and behavioural profile of your ideal customer — richer than most businesses in Nepal have ever created. Use it to guide every piece of communication you produce.

Common Mistakes to Avoid
✗
Creating a profile and then never using it
A customer profile is not a one-time exercise. It is a reference document. Every time you write a post, send a WhatsApp message, or plan a promotion, open your profile and ask: "Would this specific person care about this? Does this speak to what they want and fear?" If not, change it until it does.

A common response is: "My customers are everyone — men, women, old, young, rich, poor." This is almost never true. Your BEST customers are a specific kind of person. When you try to speak to everyone, you end up speaking to no one. Make your profile specific, even if it feels uncomfortable to narrow it down.

Lesson 03 · Competitor Intelligence
Study Your Competition & Find Your Winning Edge
⏱ 26 min🏆 Competitive Strategy📍 Work in any market
03
🎯
Lesson Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will know exactly how to analyse your competition using AI — identifying their strengths, their weaknesses, and the specific gap in the market that only your business can fill.
📖
Full Lesson

Sun Tzu — a famous general who wrote the most studied strategy book in history 2,500 years ago — said: "Know your enemy as you know yourself, and you need not fear the outcome of a hundred battles." This principle is just as true for a clothing shop in Birgunj as it was for armies in ancient China.

The businesses that thrive are not necessarily better than their competitors in every way. They are simply better at understanding where their competitors are weak — and positioning themselves to fill that gap. Competitive intelligence is not spying. It is smart observation and analysis.

In Nepal's small business market, most competitors are remarkably similar to each other. They sell the same products, at similar prices, with similar marketing, making the same claims ("best quality, lowest price, reliable service"). The ones who stand out are those who find one or two specific ways to be genuinely different — and then communicate those differences clearly.

AI helps you with this in a powerful way. While AI cannot browse your specific local competitor's Facebook page in real time, it has extensive knowledge of competitive strategies, common business weaknesses, market positioning frameworks, and differentiation tactics across every type of business. You bring the local context. AI brings the strategic framework. Together, you get insights that would cost thousands of rupees from a business consultant.

The 3 Questions of Competitor Analysis

Question 1 — What are they doing well? What is making customers go to them instead of you? Be honest. This is information you need, not a reason to feel bad. Understanding their strengths helps you decide whether to match them or differentiate around them.

Question 2 — Where are they weak? What complaints do their customers have? What type of customer are they NOT serving well? What do they ignore, do poorly, or not offer at all? Every gap in their service is an opportunity for you.

Question 3 — What could YOU do that they genuinely cannot? Because of your location, your personality, your specific skills, your relationships, your history in the community, or your unique products — what is possible for you that would be hard for them to copy?

When you can answer Question 3 clearly, you have found your winning edge. Everything in your marketing should then communicate that edge loudly and consistently.

🏆
Competitor Analysis Prompts That Deliver Real Strategy
Competitor Intelligence Prompts
// Understanding the competitive landscape
→ "I run a small pharmacy in a mid-size Nepali city. There is a large, well-stocked pharmacy 500 metres away and a cheaper pharmacy 200 metres away. I am caught in the middle. What are the typical weaknesses of large pharmacy chains compared to small ones, and how can a small pharmacy compete effectively? Give me 6 specific, practical strategies."
// Finding your differentiation
→ "I sell handmade lokta paper notebooks and stationery in Kathmandu. My main competitors are: (1) mass-produced cheap Chinese stationery in Asan market, (2) imported premium stationery in big malls. I am in the middle — handmade, local, but not as cheap as Chinese imports. How do I position my brand to stand clearly apart from both? What story should I tell and what customers should I target?"
// Turning a competitor's weakness into your strength
→ "In Nepal's restaurant market, what are the most common complaints customers have about mid-range restaurants? List the top 7 pain points. Then tell me how a small restaurant can turn each one into a specific competitive advantage through service, communication, or product quality."
Pro tip
After getting AI analysis, always ask: "Now tell me which of these strategies would be most relevant and effective for a business in Nepal specifically, given the local culture and market." This localises the strategic advice.
🗺️
Your Own Observation + AI Analysis: The Winning Combination
How to do a proper local competitor analysis in 2 hours
1
Observe physically (45 min): Visit 2–3 competitor businesses as a customer. Notice: How does the shop look? How are you greeted? How long do customers wait? What products are prominently displayed? What are the prices? Take notes on your phone — not as a spy, just as a curious observer. Every detail matters.
2
Observe digitally (20 min): Look at their Facebook page. How often do they post? What types of posts get the most reactions? What do customers comment or complain about? What do they seem to be offering that attracts customers? Screenshot the most successful content you see.
3
Ask AI to analyse your observations (15 min): Describe what you found to ChatGPT: "I visited my competitor and observed [your specific findings]. Based on this, what are their likely strengths and weaknesses? What gaps are they leaving open that I could fill? What 3 things could I do differently to attract customers they are not fully serving?"
4
Define your one clear difference (30 min): Based on your observations and AI analysis, write one sentence that clearly states how your business is different. It should be specific, believable, and something your competitor genuinely cannot easily copy. This becomes the core message in all your marketing.

Lesson 04 · Data Made Simple
Your Numbers Are Telling You Something — Let AI Translate Them
⏱ 30 min📊 Data Analysis💡 No spreadsheet skills needed
04
🎯
Lesson Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to take any business numbers — sales figures, customer counts, Facebook engagement stats — paste them into ChatGPT, and get a plain-language explanation of what they mean and exactly what you should do about them.
📖
Full Lesson

Many business owners have a relationship with their numbers that goes something like this: they avoid looking at them. The monthly revenue is noted somewhere, but what it means, why it is up or down, which products are making money and which are not — this is usually a mystery. And because it feels complicated, it stays unexamined.

This is one of the most expensive habits in small business. Numbers are not there to confuse you. They are there to tell you where the problems are and where the opportunities are — before they become obvious and too late.

The good news is: you do not need to be good at maths to understand your business data. You never did. What you need is someone to translate the numbers into plain language — to tell you the story the numbers are trying to tell. That is exactly what AI does for data.

In this lesson, you will learn to use ChatGPT as your personal data analyst. You will paste in raw numbers — your actual business data — and ask AI to tell you what it sees. You do not need spreadsheet skills. You do not need to understand formulas. You just need to be able to collect your numbers and type them in.

What data can you analyse with AI?

Monthly sales by product or category: Which products are growing? Which are declining? Which should you stock more of, and which should you quietly stop selling?
Customer visit patterns: Which days are busiest? Which times of day? When do people stop coming? This tells you when to have more staff, more stock, and when to run promotions.
Facebook page insights: Which posts got the most reach, likes, comments, and shares? What content is working? What is being ignored? What time of day should you post?
WhatsApp enquiries: What are people asking about most? What questions come up repeatedly? What objections do people raise? This is pure gold for improving your communication and product range.
Expense tracking: Where is your money going? Which costs are growing? Which could be reduced? Where is most of your revenue actually being spent?

You do not need all of these. Start with just one type of data. Even one month of sales figures, given to AI for analysis, will reveal patterns you had never consciously noticed — patterns that are worth real money.

Nepal Example — Bijay's Restaurant Data Revelation, Pokhara
🇳🇵
Bijay Rana, 44 — Restaurant Owner, Lakeside Pokhara
Data analysis revealed he was losing Rs 40,000 per month — and he had no idea

Bijay has run a mid-range restaurant at Pokhara's Lakeside for seven years, serving both tourists and locals. He tracked his monthly revenue roughly, but had never analysed it carefully. He felt the business was "doing okay but not growing."

In this lesson, he collected 6 months of sales data and broke it down by day of the week and by dish category. When he pasted this data into ChatGPT and asked for analysis, three things emerged that shocked him.

First: Tuesday and Wednesday evenings were consistently his lowest sales days — but he was paying full staff wages regardless. He was losing money on those two evenings every week. Second: his most ordered dish (dal bhat set at Rs 350) was actually his lowest-margin item — he barely made profit on it after accounting for ingredients, cooking time, and serving. Third: his tourist customers who ordered Western food (pasta, pizza, sandwiches) spent an average of 2.3x more per visit than his local customers ordering Nepali food.

"I had been working seven days a week for seven years and never noticed these patterns — they were right there in my numbers. In two months, I reduced Tuesday and Wednesday staff, repriced my dal bhat slightly, and started promoting my tourist menu more actively. My monthly profit increased by roughly Rs 40,000. I was not working harder. I was working smarter."

+Rs 40,000/month in profit · No new customers needed · No new products · Just smarter decisions from data

⚠️
Common Mistakes to Avoid
✗
Waiting until you have "perfect" data before analysing
Many business owners say "I'll start tracking properly from next month" — and next month never comes. Start with whatever data you have, even if it is rough estimates. Rough data analysed is infinitely more useful than perfect data never collected. Start now, improve your data tracking as you go.
✗
Only looking at total revenue and ignoring everything else
Total revenue tells you very little. Which product? Which day? Which type of customer? Which marketing channel brought them? Revenue broken down into these dimensions reveals the entire story of your business. The goal is to track at least 2–3 dimensions of your sales, not just the total number.

Full Lesson

Every business owner, at some point, lies awake at night thinking about a decision they cannot make. Should I hire someone? Should I move my shop to a better location? Should I raise my prices? Should I start selling online? Should I close on Tuesdays to save costs? Should I take a loan to expand?

These decisions are hard because they involve uncertainty, risk, and consequences that affect your livelihood and your family. And in Nepal's business culture, there are often additional pressures: family expectations, fear of failure being judged by the community, limited access to professional advice, and financial constraints that make a wrong decision very costly.

Most people resolve difficult decisions in one of three poor ways: they avoid the decision for months (paralysis), they make a snap decision based on emotion or a random conversation (impulsiveness), or they just copy what a competitor is doing without analysis (imitation). None of these approaches systematically lead to good outcomes.

AI does not make decisions for you — and it should not. Your life, your context, your risk tolerance, and your judgment are irreplaceable. But AI is extraordinarily good at one thing: helping you think through a decision completely, from all angles, before you commit. It surfaces factors you had not considered. It stress-tests your assumptions. It presents the downside scenarios you would prefer not to think about. And it helps you find information that reduces the uncertainty.

In this lesson, you will learn four specific frameworks for using AI to make better business decisions. Each one is appropriate for different situations — and all of them will make you a noticeably clearer thinker.

⚖️
Four AI Decision Frameworks
🔄 Framework 1: The Full Scenario Analysis
Use when: You are considering a significant change — new location, new product line, pricing change, staff hire.

Ask AI to walk through what happens if you do it AND what happens if you don't. What are the best-case, realistic-case, and worst-case outcomes for each?
"I am considering [decision]. Walk me through realistic best, moderate, and worst-case scenarios for both choosing to do it and choosing not to. Be honest about risks."
🔍 Framework 2: The Assumption Challenger
Use when: You are excited about an idea and want to make sure you are not missing something important. Use this when you feel strongly positive about a choice — it is a reality check.

Ask AI to challenge every assumption behind your reasoning.
"I want to [decision]. I am assuming [your reasons]. Please challenge each of my assumptions. What might I be wrong about? What am I not considering?"
📋 Framework 3: The Decision Checklist
Use when: You are overwhelmed by a decision and don't know where to start. Ask AI to create a structured list of everything you need to know, check, or do before making the decision confidently.

Turns chaos into a clear process.
"I need to decide whether to [decision]. Create a complete checklist of everything I should investigate, verify, and consider before making this decision responsibly."
👁️ Framework 4: The Outside Perspective
Use when: You are too emotionally involved in a decision and need to see it more objectively. Ask AI to analyse your situation as if it were advising someone else — removing the emotion.

Gives you the view of a calm, experienced advisor.
"A business owner in Nepal in exactly my situation [describe it] is considering [decision]. As an experienced business advisor, what would you tell them? What are they probably not seeing clearly?"
💡
The most important rule of AI-assisted decision making Use AI to prepare yourself — not to replace your judgment. After running one or more of these frameworks, step away from the screen for 24 hours. Let the information settle. Sleep on it. Then decide. Decisions made after reflection are almost always better than decisions made in the moment, even with perfect information.

Nepal Example — Sushila's Loan Decision, Butwal
🇳🇵
Sushila Thapa, 46 — Clothing & Tailoring Shop Owner, Butwal
How AI helped her avoid a Rs 3 lakh loan decision that would have hurt her

Sushila has been running her tailoring and clothing shop in Butwal for eleven years. Her nephew had been excitedly telling her for months that she should take a bank loan of Rs 3,00,000 to expand her shop, hire two additional tailors, and stock more high-end fabric to capture the wedding market.

She was tempted — the idea sounded exciting — but something felt wrong. She could not quite articulate what. On the advice of a fellow student in this course, she ran the Assumption Challenger framework with ChatGPT, listing all her nephew's reasons and her own assumptions about the expansion.

The AI response identified several things she had not fully thought through: the wedding market in mid-size Nepali cities is highly seasonal (concentrated around specific auspicious months), meaning 8 months of the year she would have high-end fabric sitting unsold. Hiring two additional tailors before establishing new clients for them creates a fixed cost that must be paid whether or not work comes in. And a bank loan interest of 12–15% per year on Rs 3 lakh adds roughly Rs 36,000–45,000 per year in costs before she earns an extra rupee of profit.

"I did not say no to the idea — I said not yet, not this way. Instead, I accepted one or two high-end wedding clients on commission from a wedding hall I know, to test whether I actually enjoy and can handle that market before investing heavily in it. If the test works, I will expand carefully. ChatGPT helped me see what my excitement was covering up."

Avoided a potentially damaging financial commitment · Found a risk-free way to test the idea first

Your Exercise
⚖️
Tackle Your Pending Decision Today

Every person reading this has at least one business decision they have been postponing. Today, you will use AI to make real progress on it.

  1. Name the decision. Write it down in one clear sentence: "I need to decide whether to ____."
  2. Choose the most relevant framework from the four above. If you are excited about an idea, use Framework 2 (Assumption Challenger). If you feel overwhelmed, use Framework 3 (Decision Checklist). If you feel too emotionally involved, use Framework 4 (Outside Perspective).
  3. Run the framework with ChatGPT. Give it maximum context about your specific situation, your financial reality, and what outcome you are hoping for.
  4. After reading the response, write down: What did I learn that I did not know before? What assumption was I making that might be wrong? What is one thing I can do in the next week to gather more information before deciding?
  5. Set a specific deadline for making the final decision — not today, but within 7–14 days. Research without a deadline is just procrastination with good intentions.
🏆 The goal of this exercise is not to make the decision right now. It is to stop being afraid of it. Knowledge reduces fear. By the end of this exercise, you will feel clearer and calmer about the decision you have been avoiding.

📖
Full Lesson

Here is the uncomfortable truth about business goals. Most small business owners in Nepal — and everywhere in the world — set goals like this: "I want to earn more this year." "I want to grow my business." "I want to finally start selling online." And then... nothing changes. The year passes, the goals remain unmet, and the cycle repeats.

Why? Not because of lack of desire. Not because of lack of effort. But because a goal without a concrete plan is just a wish. Wishes are not a business strategy.

A real plan has three things that wishes don't: specific, measurable targets ("increase monthly revenue by Rs 15,000 by September"), a clear set of actions you will take to reach them (not vague ideas but concrete weekly steps), and accountability — a way to track whether you are on course and what to adjust when you are not.

Building this kind of plan used to require a consultant, a business education, or years of experience. Today, with everything you have learned in this module — your research skills, your customer profile, your competitor analysis, your data understanding, and your decision frameworks — you can build a professional 90-day plan entirely on your own, using AI as your thinking partner.

And 90 days is the perfect time frame. Long enough to make real progress. Short enough to stay motivated and adapt. Three months from today, you will look at this plan and see exactly what you did, what you avoided, and what you want to do differently in the next 90 days. This is how businesses improve — not in dramatic sudden leaps, but in three-month cycles of intentional, planned improvement.

The Five Components of a 90-Day Plan

Component 1 — Your Starting Point: Where are you right now? Revenue, customers, main products/services, biggest strength, biggest weakness. Be honest — this is your baseline, not a judgement.
Component 2 — Your Single Goal: What ONE metric do you most want to change in 90 days? One goal, not five. One focused goal achieved is worth ten scattered goals that go nowhere.
Component 3 — Your Strategies: What 2–3 approaches will drive that change? These are your big moves for the quarter.
Component 4 — Your Weekly Actions: What specific things will you do each week? These are the small, daily habits and tasks that add up to big results.
Component 5 — Your Review System: How will you check progress? Weekly? Monthly? What will you do if you are behind at the halfway mark?

Common Mistakes to Avoid
✗
Making the plan perfect instead of making it real
Students sometimes spend 3 hours refining their 90-day plan, making it beautifully detailed and comprehensive — and then never start executing it because the execution feels messy compared to the perfect plan. A good plan executed imperfectly beats a perfect plan that stays on paper. Start on Day 1 of your plan, even if things are not perfectly set up yet.
✗
Setting a goal you cannot measure
"I want to grow my business" is not a 90-day goal. "I want to increase my monthly revenue by Rs 8,000 by December 31st" is. "I want more customers on Facebook" is vague. "I want 500 followers on Facebook by December 31st, with an average post reaching 300 people" is specific and measurable. Always attach a number and a date to your goal.
✗
Quitting the plan at Week 3 when it gets hard
Almost every 90-day plan hits a difficult patch around weeks 3 and 4. The initial excitement has worn off, results are not yet visible, and old habits are pulling you back. This is not a sign the plan is wrong. This is completely normal. Push through weeks 3 and 4 and you will almost certainly see real results by week 6 or 7. The people who give up at week 3 never find out that success was just 2 weeks away.

Questions About This Module

Real questions from real students about using AI for research and business decisions.

"ChatGPT does not know about Nepal specifically — how can I trust its advice?" +
This is a very fair concern, and it is why we always say: use AI for frameworks and principles, not for local specifics. ChatGPT knows business strategy, customer psychology, pricing principles, competitive positioning, and planning frameworks that are universally applicable. You provide the local context — your specific prices, your neighbourhood, your specific customers. When you combine AI's strategic knowledge with YOUR local knowledge, you get something genuinely powerful and highly relevant to Nepal.
"I don't have much business data. Can I still benefit from the data lesson?" +
Absolutely — and in fact, one of the most valuable outcomes of this lesson for people with little data is that it teaches you what data to start collecting. After running even a simple analysis with rough estimates, ask ChatGPT: "What data should I start tracking weekly to make better decisions about my business?" You will get a clear, practical list. Start tracking that data today, and in 3 months you will have real numbers to analyse. Everyone starts somewhere — starting with rough estimates is a completely valid beginning.
"What if my competitor research shows my competition is much stronger than me?" +
Then you have incredibly valuable information that most small business owners never get. Knowing your competitor is stronger is not a reason to give up — it is a reason to be strategic. Every strong competitor has weaknesses; size and strength often create inflexibility, impersonal service, and gaps in what they offer to certain customer types. Ask AI specifically: "My competitor is larger, better-resourced, and better-known than me. What are the typical weaknesses of larger, established businesses that smaller, newer businesses can exploit?" The answers will surprise you.
"The 90-day plan feels too ambitious for where I am right now. What should I do?" +
This is actually something to tell ChatGPT directly. Add this to your plan prompt: "My situation is that I am very early-stage and my resources are very limited. Please make this plan as simple and low-risk as possible. Focus on the 3 most important actions and nothing else." A good 90-day plan for someone just starting should be simple and achievable — not impressive-looking. Three well-executed small actions beat ten ambitious actions that overwhelm you. ChatGPT will adapt the plan to your reality if you give it that context.
"Can I use AI to help plan for a business I want to start — not one I already have?" +
Absolutely — and this module is arguably even more valuable for people planning to start than for those already running a business. Research your market before investing. Understand your target customer before spending on stock or marketing. Analyse potential competitors before choosing a location or product range. Make key decisions using the frameworks before committing any money. All four frameworks and both research approaches work equally well for planning a new business. You are essentially stress-testing your business idea before it costs you anything.
"Should I share my business plans and data with ChatGPT? Is it safe?" +
For day-to-day planning and analysis, sharing general business information with ChatGPT is very similar to discussing your business with a trusted advisor or family member — reasonable and practical. However, there are specific things to avoid: do not paste complete customer databases with personal information, do not share highly sensitive financial records, and do not paste information that you would not want in a public conversation. Describing your business type, rough revenue figures, and strategic situation is completely fine and common practice for business owners worldwide.
